How to Dilute Cleaner for a 24 oz Spray Bottle

A 24 oz spray bottle holds about 710 mL. That makes it slightly smaller than a 750 mL bottle and larger than a 16 oz bottle, so the concentrate amount needs to be scaled carefully instead of guessed.

12 min read • Spray bottle dilution

Part of the main guide

This article belongs to the Cleaning Dilution Guide. If you are comparing bottle sizes, also see how to dilute cleaner for a 16 oz spray bottle, how to dilute cleaner for a 32 oz spray bottle, how much concentrate for a 750 mL spray bottle, and how much concentrate for 1 liter.

Quick answer

A 24 oz spray bottle is about 710 mL. For a 1:10 dilution, use about 65 mL of concentrate and fill with water to the 24 oz mark. For 1:20, use about 34 mL. For 1:64, use about 11 mL.

The safest simple method is: measure the concentrate first, pour it into the empty bottle, then add water until the final liquid level reaches 24 oz. Do not fill the bottle with 24 oz of water first and then add concentrate, because that changes the final volume.

For any ratio not listed here, use the Cleaning Dilution Calculator and enter 24 fl oz or about 710 mL as the final bottle size.

Start with the product label, not the bottle size

The bottle size only tells you how much final solution you want. The product label tells you how strong that solution should be. Some labels use ratios like 1:10, 1:32, or 1:64. Others use mL per liter, ounces per gallon, tablespoons per gallon, or capfuls per bucket.

Do not mix those formats together. A label that says 2 oz per gallon is not the same instruction as a 1:64 ratio, even if the final amount looks close in some bottle sizes. Use the format the label gives you.

If the wording on the label is confusing, read How to Read Cleaning Dilution Instructions on Labels. If you want the math behind ratio labels, use How to Calculate Dilution Ratio.

The 24 oz spray bottle formula

For US bottle sizes, 24 fl oz is the final bottle volume. In metric, that is about 710 mL. You can calculate the concentrate amount in ounces or mL, but do not mix both systems halfway through.

When a label says 1:20, it usually means 1 part concentrate + 20 parts water. That makes 21 total parts. Divide the final bottle size by 21 to get the concentrate amount.

Step What to do Example: 1:20 in a 24 oz bottle
1 Add the ratio parts together 1 + 20 = 21 total parts
2 Divide the final bottle size by total parts 24 ÷ 21 = 1.14 fl oz concentrate
3 Convert if needed 1.14 fl oz is about 34 mL
4 Add water to the final mark Fill with water until the bottle reaches 24 oz

If your spray bottle has both oz and mL marks, choose one system and stay with it for the whole mix.

If the label says mL per liter

A 24 oz bottle is about 0.71 liter. So if the label gives a dose per liter, multiply that dose by 0.71.

Example: if the label says 20 mL per liter, use about 14 mL of concentrate in a 24 oz bottle, then add water to the final 24 oz mark.

If the label says oz per gallon

A 24 oz bottle is 3/16 of a gallon, because one US gallon is 128 fl oz. That means you multiply the label’s oz-per-gallon dose by 0.1875.

Example: if the label says 2 oz per gallon, use about 0.38 fl oz of concentrate in a 24 oz bottle. That is about 11 mL.

Do not make a full gallon just because the label uses gallons. Scale the amount down to the bottle you are actually filling. For more examples, read How to Scale oz per Gallon Cleaning Labels and What Does 2 oz per Gallon Mean?.

Step-by-step: how to mix a 24 oz spray bottle

  1. Read the product label and find the exact dilution instruction.
  2. Confirm the label format: ratio, mL per liter, oz per gallon, or another format.
  3. Measure the concentrate using a marked cup, syringe-style measure, or accurate dosing tool.
  4. Pour the concentrate into the empty 24 oz spray bottle.
  5. Add water until the final liquid level reaches the 24 oz mark.
  6. Cap the bottle and gently invert it a few times. Avoid hard shaking if the product foams.
  7. Label the bottle with the product name, dilution, and date if you will store it.

The key is final volume. You are not adding concentrate to 24 oz of water. You are making 24 oz total solution. That small difference keeps the dilution accurate.

24 oz vs 16 oz, 32 oz, 750 mL, and 1 liter

A 24 oz bottle sits in the middle of several common bottle sizes. It is larger than 16 oz, smaller than 32 oz, slightly smaller than 750 mL, and much smaller than 1 liter. That means you should not copy the amount from another bottle unless you scale it.

Bottle size Approx. mL Compared with 24 oz
16 oz 473 mL 24 oz needs 1.5× the 16 oz amount
24 oz 710 mL Current article
750 mL 750 mL Slightly more than 24 oz
32 oz 946 mL 24 oz needs 75% of the 32 oz amount
1 liter 1000 mL 24 oz needs about 71% of the 1 liter amount

For nearby sizes, use the 16 oz guide, the 32 oz guide, the 750 mL guide, or the 1 liter guide.

Cleaner vs disinfectant: the label decides

For a general cleaner, dilution affects cleaning strength, residue, streaking, smell, and waste. Too much concentrate can leave a sticky finish. Too little may not clean well.

For a disinfectant, dilution is only one part of correct use. The label may also require a pre-cleaned surface, a wet contact time, a certain surface type, ventilation, rinsing, or a fresh solution. A random spray bottle ratio is not enough.

If your goal is disinfection, read Cleaning vs Disinfecting: What Is the Difference? before mixing. For bleach specifically, start with the Bleach Dilution Guide.

Common 24 oz dilution mistakes

  • Using the 16 oz amount: this makes the 24 oz bottle too weak.
  • Using the 32 oz amount: this makes the 24 oz bottle too strong.
  • Filling with water first: 24 oz of water plus concentrate is more than 24 oz total solution.
  • Guessing small doses: ratios like 1:64 and 1:128 need careful measuring.
  • Ignoring surface limits: dilution does not make every cleaner safe for stone, wood, painted surfaces, glass, or fabric.
  • Mixing products together: do not combine cleaners unless the product label clearly says to do it.

For safety examples, see Can You Mix Bleach and Vinegar?, Can You Mix Bleach and Ammonia?, and Can You Mix Vinegar and Hydrogen Peroxide?.

FAQs

  • How much concentrate do I use for a 24 oz spray bottle? It depends on the label dilution. At 1:10, use about 65 mL. At 1:20, use about 34 mL. At 1:64, use about 11 mL.
  • Is a 24 oz spray bottle the same as 750 mL? No. A 24 oz bottle is about 710 mL. A 750 mL bottle is slightly larger, so it needs slightly more concentrate for the same ratio.
  • Can I use the 16 oz dilution amount in a 24 oz bottle? No. A 24 oz bottle needs 1.5 times the 16 oz amount for the same dilution.
  • Can I use the 32 oz dilution amount in a 24 oz bottle? No. A 24 oz bottle needs 75% of the 32 oz amount for the same dilution.
  • Should I add water or concentrate first? Add the measured concentrate first, then add water until the bottle reaches the final 24 oz mark, unless the product label gives a different order.
  • Can I use this chart for bleach? Do not use a general cleaner chart for bleach. Use the bleach product label and read How to Dilute Bleach for a Spray Bottle.
