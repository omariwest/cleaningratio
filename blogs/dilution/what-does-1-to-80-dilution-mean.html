What Does a 1:80 Dilution Mean? Exact mL + oz Examples

A 1:80 dilution usually means 1 part cleaner concentrate mixed with 80 parts water. That gives 81 total parts, so the amount of concentrate is small and should be measured carefully.

12 min read • Ratio dilution guide

Part of the main guide

This article belongs to the Cleaning Dilution Guide. If you are comparing ratios, also see what a 1:40 dilution means, how to calculate dilution ratio, how much concentrate for a 750 mL spray bottle, and how much concentrate for a 2 liter bottle.

Quick answer

A 1:80 dilution usually means 1 part concentrate + 80 parts water. That makes 81 total parts. To calculate the concentrate amount, divide the final amount of mixed solution by 81.

For example, a 1:80 dilution in a 1 liter bottle needs about 12 mL concentrate. A 750 mL spray bottle needs about 9 mL concentrate. A 1 gallon mix needs about 1.6 fl oz concentrate.

Need another size? Use the Cleaning Dilution Calculator and enter 1:80 plus your final bottle or bucket volume.

What 1:80 means on a cleaner label

On most cleaning concentrate labels, 1:80 means one part concentrated product to eighty parts water. The finished solution has 81 total parts, not 80 total parts.

This matters because 1:80 is already a light dilution. In small spray bottles, the concentrate amount may be under 10 mL. Guessing by splash, capful, or quick pour can easily make the solution too strong.

If the product label defines the ratio differently, follow the label. But for most cleaner dilution examples, 1:80 = 1 concentrate + 80 water.

1:80 dilution formula

Since 1:80 has 81 total parts, divide the final solution size by 81. That gives the concentrate amount.

Step What to do Example for 1 liter
1 Add the parts together 1 + 80 = 81 total parts
2 Divide final volume by 81 1000 mL ÷ 81 = 12.3 mL
3 Measure that much concentrate About 12 mL concentrate
4 Add water to final volume Fill with water to 1 liter total

Do not fill the bottle with the full amount of water first. Add concentrate first, then add water until the final volume reaches the bottle or bucket mark.

How to mix a 1:80 cleaning solution

  1. Read the product label and confirm that 1:80 is the correct dilution.
  2. Choose the final amount you want to make, such as 500 mL, 750 mL, 1 liter, 24 oz, 1 gallon, or 3 gallons.
  3. Divide the final volume by 81 to get the concentrate amount.
  4. Measure the concentrate carefully. For spray bottles, a syringe-style measure or small dosing cup is better than a capful.
  5. Pour the concentrate into the empty bottle or bucket.
  6. Add water until the final solution reaches the correct volume mark.
  7. Mix gently. Do not shake hard if the cleaner foams.
  8. Label the container if the solution will be stored.

The measuring step matters more at 1:80 than at stronger ratios. In a 750 mL bottle, the difference between 9 mL and 15 mL is not small. It can turn a light dilution into a noticeably stronger mix.

Real examples

Example 1: 1:80 dilution for a 500 mL spray bottle

Add 1 + 80 to get 81 total parts. Divide 500 mL by 81. The result is about 6.2 mL concentrate. Add about 6 mL concentrate first, then fill with water to 500 mL.

Example 2: 1:80 dilution for a 750 mL spray bottle

Divide 750 mL by 81. The result is about 9.3 mL concentrate. Add about 9 mL concentrate to the empty bottle, then add water until the final volume reaches 750 mL.

Example 3: 1:80 dilution for a 1 liter bottle

One liter is 1000 mL. Divide 1000 by 81. The result is 12.3 mL concentrate. Measure about 12 mL concentrate, then add water to the 1 liter mark.

Example 4: 1:80 dilution for 1 gallon

One US gallon is 128 fl oz. Divide 128 by 81. The result is about 1.58 fl oz concentrate. Add about 1.6 fl oz concentrate, then add water until the final solution reaches 1 gallon.

Example 5: 1:80 dilution for a 3 gallon bucket

Three gallons is 384 fl oz. Divide 384 by 81. The result is about 4.74 fl oz concentrate. Add that to the bucket first, then add water to the 3 gallon mark.

1:80 is not the same as 1:40, 1:64, or 1:100

Ratios that look close are not interchangeable. A 1:40 dilution is about twice as strong as 1:80. A 1:64 dilution is stronger than 1:80. A 1:100 dilution is weaker than 1:80.

Ratio Concentrate for 1 liter Compared with 1:80

1:64 15.4 mL Stronger than 1:80
1:80 12.3 mL Current article

Do not switch to a nearby dilution unless the product label gives that option. If you want the stronger comparison, read What Does a 1:40 Dilution Mean?.

Where a 1:80 dilution is often used

A 1:80 dilution is often used when a product is concentrated and the job does not need a heavy-soil mix. It may appear on labels for general surface cleaners, floor cleaners, maintenance cleaning, refill systems, and light-duty cleaning tasks.

For floors, a 1:80 mix may help reduce residue compared with stronger ratios, but it still depends on the product label and floor type. If the floor feels sticky after drying, the mix may still be too strong, the mop water may be dirty, or the product may need rinsing.

For floor-specific help, read How to Dilute Floor Cleaner and How to Dilute Floor Cleaner Without Sticky Residue.

Best way to measure a 1:80 dilution

Because 1:80 uses a small amount of concentrate, the measuring tool matters. A large measuring cup may be fine for gallons or buckets, but it is not ideal for a 500 mL or 750 mL spray bottle.

Container size Approx. concentrate Better measuring tool
500 mL 6 mL Syringe-style measure or precise spoon
750 mL 9 mL Syringe-style measure or small dosing cup
1 liter 12 mL Small dosing cup
1 gallon 1.6 fl oz Marked measuring cup
3 gallons 4.7 fl oz Marked measuring cup or jug

Avoid capfuls unless the label specifically defines the cap size and tells you to use it. Caps vary, and a small cap error can matter a lot in light dilutions.

Cleaner vs disinfectant at 1:80

For a general cleaner, 1:80 affects cleaning strength, residue, smell, streaking, and cost. Too much concentrate can leave film. Too little may not clean well.

For a disinfectant, the dilution alone is not enough. The label may also require a specific contact time, surface type, pre-cleaning, rinsing, ventilation, or fresh solution. Do not assume a product disinfects at 1:80 unless the label says that use is approved.

If your goal is disinfection, read Cleaning vs Disinfecting: What Is the Difference?. For bleach, use the Bleach Dilution Guide instead of a general 1:80 chart.

Common 1:80 dilution mistakes

  • Thinking 1:80 means 80 total parts: for most cleaner labels, it means 1 part concentrate plus 80 parts water, or 81 total parts.
  • Guessing tiny amounts: spray bottles often need less than 10–12 mL concentrate at this ratio.
  • Using the 1:40 amount: this makes the solution about twice as strong as intended.
  • Adding concentrate to a full bottle: this changes the final volume and the dilution.
  • Ignoring surface instructions: the right dilution does not make every cleaner safe for every material.
  • Mixing products together: do not combine cleaners unless the label clearly allows it.

For unsafe product combinations, read Can You Mix Bleach and Vinegar?, Can You Mix Bleach and Ammonia?, and Can You Mix Vinegar and Hydrogen Peroxide?.

FAQs

  • What does 1:80 dilution mean? It usually means 1 part concentrate mixed with 80 parts water, making 81 total parts.
  • How much concentrate is needed for 1 liter at 1:80? Use about 12 mL concentrate, then add water until the final solution reaches 1 liter.
  • How much concentrate is needed for 750 mL at 1:80? Use about 9 mL concentrate, then fill with water to 750 mL.
  • How much concentrate is needed for 1 gallon at 1:80? Use about 1.6 fl oz concentrate, then fill with water to 1 gallon.
  • Is 1:80 stronger than 1:40? No. A 1:80 dilution uses about half as much concentrate as a 1:40 dilution for the same final volume.
  • Is 1:80 stronger than 1:100? Yes. A 1:80 dilution uses more concentrate than a 1:100 dilution.
  • Can I use a 1:80 chart for bleach? Do not use a general cleaner chart for bleach. Use the bleach product label and the Bleach Dilution Guide.
